Mob Lynching - Don’t hate what you don’t understand

Mob Lynching refers to killing someone with an “alleged” offence without following any due process of law. Misunderstanding arising from ignorance breeds fear and fear is the greatest enemy of peace. It took the United States over 100 years to declare lynching a federal crime. Unfortunately, over the last few years, we have seen a drastic increase in number of mob lynchings in India. The mob lynchings have increased in the name of religion, child kidnapping and cow slaughter.

Let us look at a few examples to understand what this is all about -

  • Mohammed Akhlaq of Dadri was accused of stealing and slaughtering a calf for Eid. Later that day, a mob was gathered that proceeded to Akhlaq’s house. The leftover meat curry in the fridge was considered a proof and Akhlaq and his son Danish were beaten with bricks and rods. Akhlaq succumbed to those injuries while Danish had to undergo a brain surgery. Despite the horrendous crime, all the accused have got bail and are out of jail.

  • After a fourteen year old went missing, a mentally ill woman was lynched in West Bengal after a rumour spread that the Bangladeshi child kidnappers were active in the area.

  • A recent case of Tabrez Ansari, where the videos of him were circulated in which he was forced to recite “Jai Shri Ram” and was being beaten with rods and sticks. His skull was cracked and bones were broken. The police took him to a local healthcare where the first aid was provided but he later died of his injuries four days later. As far as the justice is concerned, the post mortem reports suggests that he died of cardiac arrest.

How can we tackle this?

We need to have stricter laws for the “fake news” that is spread through WhatsApp. With the availability of cheap internet, a lot of people have access to baseless fake news. Mob lynching cases should be fast-tracked and should be dealt with utmost strictness. The citizens need to inform the police immediately in case of a mob attack and the police should be active enough to disperse the mob immediately.

Quoting Martin Luther - “It may be true that the law can not make a man love me, but it can stop him from lynching me and that I think is pretty important”.
